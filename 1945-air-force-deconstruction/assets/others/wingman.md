When you start the game, you will get a Tier 1 wingman for free at level 2, this is the P47 Thunderbolt. As you progress in the game you get more wingman through completing special events and in-app purchases.

There are 2 Tiers of wingman, Tier 1 has 16 wingman to choose from, Tier 2 has 8 wingman which you get by merging 2 dedicated Tier 1 wingman together. Once Tier 3 wingman will be released, there will be 4 Tier 3 wingman which you can get by merging 2 dedicated Tier 2 wingman.

Each Wingman has 3 different stats that are often referenced when discussing superiority of one plane to another. Please note there is little evidence to suggest these stats have any real differences while playing the game. These 3 stats are: Power, Damage Per Second and Damage Per Hit. A wingman doesn't have Fuel. See Wingman Stats for a table of wingman stats.

Wingman Upgrade Basics
As you progress through the game you will need to upgrade your wingman. The strength of your wingman is determined by the number of stars you have achieved combined with the number of upgrades for each star you achieve. Each wingman starts with a predetermined number of stars and can be promoted up to 10 stars. In order to promote your wingman you must collect the required number of modules to achieve the next star. After you promote your wingman you must then upgrade using gold coins. You must upgrade your aircraft 5 times by using the gold coins you've earned or purchased in the game before you can promote it to the next star.

When you promote your aircraft to levels 3, 5 & 7 you unlock special weapons that increase your aircraft strength. See the chart below for information on what is unlocked at each level.

In The Real World
The Vought F4U Corsair is an American fighter aircraft that saw service primarily in World War II and the Korean War.

Designed and initially manufactured by Chance Vought, the Corsair was soon in great demand; additional production contracts were given to Goodyear, whose Corsairs were designated FG, and Brewster, designated F3A.

The Corsair was designed and operated as a carrier-based aircraft, and entered service in large numbers with the U.S. Navy in late 1944 and early 1945. It quickly became one of the most capable carrier-based fighter-bombers of World War II. Some Japanese pilots regarded it as the most formidable American fighter of World War II and its naval aviators achieved an 11:1 kill ratio. Early problems with carrier landings and logistics led to it being eclipsed as the dominant carrier-based fighter by the Grumman F6F Hellcat, powered by the same Double Wasp engine first flown on the Corsair's first prototype in 1940. Instead, the Corsair's early deployment was to land-based squadrons of the U.S. Marines and U.S. Navy.

The Corsair served almost exclusively as a fighter-bomber throughout the Korean War and during the French colonial wars in Indochina and Algeria. In addition to its use by the U.S. and British, the Corsair was also used by the Royal New Zealand Air Force, French Naval Aviation, and other air forces until the 1960s.

From the first prototype delivery to the U.S. Navy in 1940, to final delivery in 1953 to the French, 12,571 F4U Corsairs were manufactured in 16 separate models. Its 1942–1953 production run was the longest of any U.S. piston-engined fighter.
